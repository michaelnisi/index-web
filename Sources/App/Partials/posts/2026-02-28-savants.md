# LLMs are savants

> It's like having a savant colleague who can solve differential equations in their head but forgets where they parked their car.

– [Claude](https://claude.ai/)

I just watched Claude make the same mistake three times in five minutes. I was updating my /now page, mentioning that Xcode 26.3 finally made agentic coding possible. Claude "corrected" it to Xcode 16.3, assuming I'd made a typo. I fixed it back to 26.3. Claude apologized and changed it to 16.3 again. I explained that Apple switched to year-based versioning in 2026. Claude understood, fixed it back to 26.3, then immediately asked me what typo I'd introduced.

The thing is, I don't care. While Claude was having this small cognitive crisis, it had already helped me implement a complete sitemap.xml endpoint with proper ETag caching, fix Makefile portability issues, and refine the prose on my now page. The version number confusion was annoying, but it didn't matter because Claude isn't my memory - it's my encyclopedic technical reference and code generator.

This savant quality is precisely what makes LLMs so useful for coding. You don't need them to remember conversational context perfectly. You need them to generate correct Swift concurrency code, understand REST semantics, follow architectural patterns, and recall obscure API details. They're brilliant at structure and terrible at continuity.

The division of labor is clear: the human provides sanity, continuity, and judgment. The LLM provides encyclopedic technical knowledge and execution. It's a partnership where both parties contribute what they're actually good at. You stop expecting the AI to "understand" you and start treating it like what it is - a savant colleague who's invaluable despite (or because of) its limitations.

The future of coding isn't about AI replacing developers. It's about developers learning to work with savants.
